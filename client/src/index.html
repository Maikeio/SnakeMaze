Normal Maps

Something that hit my by suprise and I didn't thought of as a problem. But they were, so I learned about tangent space. Again, I apreciated the help of David Lettier.

Deferred lighting

One goal, since the start, was the ability to use many lights, so this wouldn't be a problem in athmospharic scenes, so I looked into deferred lighting. I was very pleased to find this handy tutorial by Joey de Vries.

"Volumetric" Fog

For the ambience a volumetric fog would be very nice, but true volumetric fog with shadow cameras and depth testing isn't an option for webgl or so many lights. So I was quiet happy when found I this simplified solution by Isaac Dykeman. Which in fact was still to expencive to render at full resoultion, so I render it at a lower resolution in an extra pass. I also was able to adapt the math to compensate for an hight limit, so the fog stays down.

Terrain

It's (obviously) a vertex shader, that displaces the verticis based on a heightmap with scrolling uvs. I blurred it a bit in the distance to reduce flickering and create normals based on the heightmap. The map is generadet with a ractal Brownian Motion and turbulences. About the last part I learned in the awesome The Book of Shaders by Patricio Gonzalez Vivo and Jen Lowe.

Skinning

A scene without a cute, or what I'm capable of to archive, character wouldn't be half as apealing, but a character needs animation. And with Three.js already bringing support for skinned animations with animation textures, I decided to go down that path with the help of this article.

Shell Texturing

At this point I started to go a step towards visiual effects. To give the Furr at least some depth, I use shell texturing. The inspiration for this effect I got by a YouTube Video by Acerola.
